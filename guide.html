What is this dashboard?

Imagine you could take everything valuable in the world — every house, every company, every government IOU, every gold bar, every bitcoin — and line it all up on one ruler. That's what this dashboard does. It shows the world's biggest pools of money to scale, so you can see with your own eyes how they compare.

The headline number is around $840 trillion. A trillion is a thousand billion — a million million. If you earned $1,000 every single day, it would take you almost 3 million years to earn one trillion dollars. The world holds roughly 840 of those.

The six asset classes

An asset is anything you own that has value. The dashboard tracks the six biggest kinds.

🏠 Real Estate — ~$393T

Every home, apartment, office, shop and warehouse on Earth, added together. It's the biggest pile of wealth by far — bigger than everything else on the dashboard combined.

Think of it this way: the flat you live in, the school you go to, the mall near you — someone owns each of those buildings, and each one has a price.

🧾 Bonds — ~$156T

A bond is an IOU. When a government or a company needs money, it borrows from investors and promises to pay it back with interest. That promise can be bought and sold, like a trading card.

Lend a friend $10 and they promise you $11 next month — you basically own a tiny bond. Now imagine that with governments borrowing trillions.

📈 Equities (Stocks) — ~$135T

A stock is a small slice of ownership in a company. Own one share of Apple and you literally own a (very tiny) piece of Apple. "Market cap" is the price of one share × all the shares — what the whole company is "worth."

If a company were a pizza cut into a billion slices, a stock is one slice — and the price of a slice changes every day depending on how hungry people are for it.

💵 Broad Money (M2) — ~$102T

All the actual money out there: coins, banknotes, and the balances in checking and savings accounts. Here's the surprising part — banks create new money whenever they make loans. That's why this number grows almost every year.

Your bank balance isn't a stack of notes in a vault with your name on it — it's a number the bank owes you. Most money today is just numbers moving between computers.

🪙 Gold — ~$28T

Humans have dug up about 220,000 tonnes of gold in all of history — it would fit in a cube about 22 meters on each side. People have trusted it as a store of value for 5,000 years, which is why its price often rises when people get nervous about everything else.

All the gold ever mined could fit inside four Olympic swimming pools — yet it's worth more than ten times all of crypto.

🌐 Crypto — ~$2.3T

Digital money that runs on blockchains — public computer networks nobody controls alone. Bitcoin is the biggest. Crypto is famous for wild swings: it has crashed by 60–80% several times and then grown back. On the world scale it's still tiny — a rounding error next to real estate.

If all the world's wealth were $100, crypto would be about 27 cents. That surprises almost everyone.

How to read the charts

  • Bars to scale — in the first section, the width of each bar is proportional to real value. Real estate spans the whole track; crypto is a thin sliver. That's the entire point: seeing size honestly.
  • The $100 split — the same data, but imagined as a single $100 bill divided up. Easier to feel than trillions.
  • Yearly trend charts — the small curve in each "Market by Market" panel shows how that asset grew (or shrank) year by year since 2015. A rising curve = the pile got bigger.
  • 30-day live sparklines — gold and crypto also show a small line of the last 30 days of live prices, refreshed daily.
  • ▲ / ▼ badges — how much a live asset moved in the last day / 30 days.
  • Log scale — some trend charts use a logarithmic scale, where each step up means "×10" instead of "+10". It's the only honest way to put slow-growing bonds and fast-growing assets on one chart.

Where the numbers come from

The dashboard has two kinds of data, and it's honest about which is which:

  • Tier 1 — live (crypto, gold): fetched automatically every day at 06:15 UTC from public APIs (CoinGecko, gold-api.com). These numbers move daily.
  • Tier 2 — curated (everything else): giant totals like "all real estate on Earth" aren't measured daily by anyone. They come from research reports (Savills, BIS, IMF, UBS…) and are reviewed monthly by hand.
  • Market Pulse & calendar: the index tiles (S&P 500, KOSPI, Nikkei…) come from Yahoo Finance each night — if a fetch fails, yesterday's value stays with a dotted underline. The economic calendar is hand-curated from official Fed, BLS, ECB and BoJ schedules, which are published months in advance.

Every figure carries an asOf date — when it was last true. If a live fetch fails, the dashboard shows the previous value with a dotted underline and a stale badge instead of pretending. Rule of the site: never fake freshness.

Big ideas behind the trends

Why does the money supply keep growing?

Banks create money by lending, and central banks add more during crises (2008, 2020). More money chasing the same goods is one reason prices creep up over time — that's inflation. It's also why "$1 in 1990" bought much more than $1 today.

Why is crypto so volatile?

It's young, it's small (so single big trades move it), and its price is mostly driven by what people believe it will be worth later. Belief changes fast; office buildings don't.

What does the "$1 invested" chart mean?

It answers: if you had put $1 into stocks (or gold, or bonds) in 1990 / 2000 / 2010 and just waited, what would it be worth now? It shows the superpower of compounding — growth on top of growth. Small yearly gains snowball over decades.

Interest rates vs. inflation

When inflation spiked to ~9% in 2022, the US central bank raised interest rates hard — making borrowing expensive to cool prices down. Watch the two lines chase each other in the Rates & Inflation chart: that chase moves almost every other price on the dashboard.

Wealth ≠ money

The $840T isn't cash sitting somewhere — it's mostly prices of things. If everyone tried to sell at once, prices would collapse. Wealth is a snapshot of what things would trade for today, not a pile you could withdraw.

Glossary

Asset
Anything you own that has value and could be sold.
Market cap
Price of one share (or coin) × how many exist. What the whole thing is "worth."
Trillion
1,000,000,000,000 — a thousand billions.
Bond
A tradeable IOU from a government or company that pays interest.
M2
A measure of money: cash + checking + savings + money-market funds.
Central bank
The institution that issues a country's currency and sets interest rates (e.g. the Fed).
FOMC
The Fed's rate-setting committee. Its eight meetings a year are the most-watched dates in finance.
NFP
Non-Farm Payrolls — the monthly US jobs report, released on the first Friday of each month.
Inflation (CPI)
How much everyday prices rose versus a year ago, in percent.
ATH
All-time high — the highest price something has ever reached.
Volatility
How wildly a price swings. High volatility = big ups and big downs.
Notional
The face value a derivative contract references — a bookkeeping number, not money at risk.
Stale
On this site: a value whose live refresh failed, shown from the last good fetch.

One last thing: this dashboard is for understanding the world, not for picking investments. Nothing here is financial advice. The best thing you can invest in at 15–18 is understanding how the machine works — which is exactly what you just did.
